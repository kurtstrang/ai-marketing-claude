# Landing Page CRO Analysis

## Skill Purpose
Perform a comprehensive Conversion Rate Optimization (CRO) analysis on Moss landing pages and campaign pages. This skill produces a section-by-section teardown with prioritized, actionable fixes that directly impact demo volume, qualified self-serve starts, and revenue pipeline.

Moss context is fixed and must not be rediscovered:
- Moss is a Germany-based spend management provider for companies with roughly 30 to 250 employees and a high share of indirect spend.
- Core buyer groups are finance leaders, finance managers, accounting teams, founders, and operations leaders in mid-market SMBs.
- Typical target industries include professional services firms such as marketing agencies, IT resellers, and similar businesses with distributed company spend.
- Moss’ core product modules are corporate credit cards, invoice management, reimbursements, budget control, integrations to accounting/HR tools, and business account/liquidity visibility.
- Moss copy should feel clear, credible, operationally smart, and finance-trustworthy — never vague, consumerish, or overly hype-driven.

## When to Use
- User provides a Moss landing page URL and asks for conversion optimization
- User asks for landing page feedback, review, or audit for getmoss.com
- User wants to improve demo bookings, qualified self-serve starts, lead capture, or pipeline generation
- Triggered by `/market landing <url>` or `/market cro <url>`

## How to Execute

### Step 1: Identify the Page Type
Determine which type of Moss landing page you are analyzing. This affects benchmark expectations and scoring weights.

| Page Type | Primary Goal | Good CR | Great CR |
|---|---|---|---|
| Demo Booking | Qualified demo request / meeting booked | 2-5% | 6%+ |
| Self-Serve Signup | Qualified account creation / get-started intent | 3-7% | 8%+ |
| Product / Solution Page | CTA clickthrough to demo or get started | 4-8% | 10%+ |
| Pricing / Comparison | Demo or get-started conversion from high-intent traffic | 5-10% | 12%+ |
| Lead Capture | Email/form submission for finance content or guides | 8-15% | 18%+ |
| Webinar Registration | Register for finance/product event | 20-30% | 35%+ |
| Industry / Persona Landing Page | Qualified demo or clickthrough from tailored audience page | 3-6% | 7%+ |
| Partner / Referral Landing Page | Demo, signup, or warm lead conversion | 10-20% | 25%+ |

### Step 2: Run the 7-Point CRO Framework
Analyze each section in order. Score each section 1-10 and provide specific findings.

#### Section 1: Hero Section (Weight: 25%)
The first screen a visitor sees. This is where the page must prove relevance to a Moss buyer fast.

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Headline is visible within 2 seconds of page load
- [ ] Headline communicates a finance outcome or operational benefit, not a generic software claim
- [ ] Headline makes the audience feel “this is for teams like mine”
- [ ] Subheadline expands with specificity around spend control, finance efficiency, visibility, or automation
- [ ] Primary CTA is above the fold and matches buying stage ("Book demo", "Get started", "Take product tour")
- [ ] CTA button color contrasts with the background
- [ ] CTA text is action-oriented and promise-matched
- [ ] Hero image or product UI supports the message (not generic stock or decorative abstraction)
- [ ] Trust signals visible above the fold (logos, proof, security/compliance, product credibility)
- [ ] Page loads in under 3 seconds
- [ ] No navigation or competing CTAs undermine the primary path on dedicated pages

**Scoring Criteria:**
- 9-10: Clear finance outcome, specific audience relevance, strong CTA, product-led visual, and trust indicators that make Moss feel credible immediately.
- 7-8: Strong hero and CTA but one meaningful gap remains (proof, specificity, product visual, or audience clarity).
- 5-6: Message is understandable but generic, broad, or too feature-led for a finance buyer.
- 3-4: Headline is vague, category-level, or does not make Moss meaningfully different or relevant.
- 1-2: No clear value, no clear audience, or no clear next step within the first screen.

#### Section 2: Value Proposition (Weight: 20%)
How clearly the page communicates WHY a Moss buyer should convert.

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Clear statement of what Moss does for finance, accounting, or operations teams
- [ ] Specific outcomes or results promised (time saved, better control, faster approvals, better visibility, cleaner accounting workflows)
- [ ] Differentiation from alternatives is clear (manual workflows, separate tools, legacy finance stacks, status-quo processes)
- [ ] Target audience is clear (visitor knows if this is for their role, company size, and use case)
- [ ] Benefits are quantified where possible
- [ ] Value proposition is scannable and easy to grasp without reading dense paragraphs

**Evaluate Using the 4U Framework:**
1. **Useful** - Does it solve a real finance or spend-management problem the visitor has?
2. **Urgent** - Is there a reason to act now, not “sometime later”?
3. **Unique** - Is it clear why Moss is a better choice than patching together cards, invoices, reimbursements, and accounting workflows?
4. **Ultra-specific** - Are claims concrete, role-relevant, and operationally believable?

#### Section 3: Social Proof (Weight: 15%)
Evidence that companies like Moss’ ICP trust and benefit from Moss.

**Types of Social Proof (ranked by persuasion power):**
1. Named customer outcomes with quantified finance or operations results
2. Named customer testimonials from CFOs, finance leads, accounting leaders, or founders
3. Recognizable customer logos that match Moss’ ICP
4. Case studies by industry, company size, or job-to-be-done
5. Product review proof (e.g. strong review platform signal) if current and credible
6. Media mentions and analyst recognition
7. Security, compliance, and operational credibility markers
8. Team size, market presence, or company maturity signals
9. Social follower counts or vanity metrics

**Checklist:**
- [ ] At least 2 types of social proof present
- [ ] Testimonials include real names, roles, and companies
- [ ] Testimonials mention concrete results, not generic praise
- [ ] Social proof is placed near decision points and CTAs
- [ ] Numbers are specific where possible
- [ ] Logos are recognizable and relevant to Moss’ buyer
- [ ] Social proof is recent, role-relevant, and believable

#### Section 4: Features and Benefits (Weight: 15%)
How the page presents what Moss includes and why it matters.

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Features are translated into benefits for finance workflows
- [ ] Content is scannable (icons, bullets, short blocks, strong mini-headlines)
- [ ] Visual hierarchy guides the eye through the most important modules first
- [ ] Most important benefits are ordered by buyer value, not by internal product org chart
- [ ] Each feature section has a clear mini-headline
- [ ] Product screenshots, product UI, or workflow visuals accompany descriptions
- [ ] The page prioritizes the most relevant 3-7 capabilities rather than dumping every Moss module equally

**Feature-to-Benefit Translation Check:**
Bad: "Invoice management"
Good: "Route, approve, and book invoices faster so finance spends less time chasing approvals and more time closing the books."

#### Section 5: Objection Handling (Weight: 10%)
How the page addresses reasons a Moss buyer might NOT convert.

**Common Objections by Page Type:**

| Objection | How to Address |
|---|---|
| "We already have cards / accounting / expense tools" | Show why Moss replaces fragmented workflows and improves control end-to-end |
| "Implementation sounds painful" | Show rollout path, onboarding support, speed-to-value, and migration clarity |
| "Will it work with our accounting or HR stack?" | Surface integrations, setup expectations, and ecosystem fit |
| "How do we control spend across teams?" | Explain approvals, budgets, policy controls, and card governance |
| "Is this secure and finance-grade?" | Show security, compliance, permissions, and control signals |
| "I’m not sure this is for a company like ours" | Use role-, industry-, and company-size-specific proof and examples |
| "I need buy-in from my finance team or management" | Provide ROI framing, case studies, and clear business-case language |

**Checklist:**
- [ ] FAQ section addresses the top 3-5 real objections for Moss buyers
- [ ] Risk reducers are present (product tour, guided demo, onboarding clarity, implementation support)
- [ ] Pricing is transparent or the reason for custom pricing is clearly justified
- [ ] Security and privacy indicators are visible where relevant
- [ ] Comparison with alternatives or status-quo workflows is addressed where useful

#### Section 6: Call-to-Action (Weight: 10%)
The conversion mechanism itself.

**CTA Button Checklist:**
- [ ] CTA text describes the next-value step, not a generic action
- [ ] CTA button is visually dominant (size, color, whitespace)
- [ ] CTA appears multiple times on long pages
- [ ] Secondary CTA exists for visitors not ready to talk to sales yet
- [ ] CTA has supporting microcopy where needed (e.g. "No credit card required", "See the product", "Talk to an expert")
- [ ] Button text matches buyer intent and awareness level
- [ ] CTA is specific to the offer and page context

**CTA Copy Scoring:**
- Weak: "Submit", "Click Here", "Learn More"
- Medium: "Book Demo", "Get Started", "Talk to Sales"
- Strong: "See How Moss Fits Our Finance Workflow", "Take the Product Tour", "Start Managing Spend with Moss"

#### Section 7: Footer and Secondary Elements (Weight: 5%)
The bottom of the page and supporting elements.

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Final CTA present at bottom of page
- [ ] Contact information, support options, or sales access is visible
- [ ] Privacy policy and terms of service linked
- [ ] Trust, security, or proof elements repeated near the final CTA
- [ ] No unnecessary links pull users away from the core conversion path
- [ ] Copyright and legal information present
- [ ] Locale links, social links, and secondary navigation support trust without distracting from conversion

### Step 3: Copy Scoring
Score the overall page copy on 5 dimensions (1-10 each):

1. **Clarity** - Can a finance buyer understand the offer in 5 seconds?
2. **Urgency** - Is there a reason to act NOW vs later?
3. **Specificity** - Are claims concrete with numbers, workflows, integrations, timeframes, or outcomes?
4. **Proof** - Are claims backed by evidence, customer outcomes, or operational detail?
5. **Action Orientation** - Does the copy drive toward a specific next step that matches buyer intent?

Calculate the Copy Score: average of all 5 dimensions, multiplied by 10 for a score out of 100.

### Step 4: Form Optimization Audit
If the page has a form, evaluate:

| Element | Best Practice |
|---|---|
| Field count | Moss demo or lead forms should ask only for routing-critical information. Keep to 4-6 essential fields when possible. |
| Labels | Use persistent labels. Avoid placeholder-only labels, especially for finance buyers filling forms quickly. |
| Button text | Match the value proposition. "Book My Demo" or "See Moss in Action" beats "Submit". |
| Error handling | Inline validation. Specific error messages. Never wipe the form after an error. |
| Multi-step | Use multi-step only when qualification is necessary; the first step should feel light and high-intent. |
| Required vs optional | Mark optional fields, not required ones. Only gate on what sales truly needs. |
| Auto-fill | Enable browser auto-fill for business-standard fields like work email, name, and company. |
| Field types | Use appropriate input types (email, tel, url) for mobile keyboards and clean data capture. |

### Step 5: Mobile Responsiveness Audit
Mobile still matters for Moss even if some buyers convert later on desktop. Check:

- [ ] CTA is thumb-reachable or easy to re-access
- [ ] Text is readable without zooming (16px minimum body text)
- [ ] Forms are usable on mobile (large tap targets, appropriate keyboards)
- [ ] Product screenshots and UI visuals resize properly and stay legible
- [ ] No horizontal scrolling required
- [ ] Page loads under 3 seconds on 4G
- [ ] Click-to-call or quick contact paths exist where relevant
- [ ] Sticky CTA bar on scroll is used only when it helps, not when it distracts

### Step 6: Page Speed Impact Assessment
Reference these conversion impact benchmarks:

| Load Time | Conversion Impact |
|---|---|
| 0-2 seconds | Baseline (optimal) |
| 2-3 seconds | -7% conversion rate |
| 3-5 seconds | -20% conversion rate |
| 5-8 seconds | -35% conversion rate |
| 8+ seconds | -50%+ conversion rate |

Check for common speed issues:
- Oversized product screenshots, hero videos, or image assets
- Render-blocking JavaScript
- Missing browser caching
- No CDN
- Excessive third-party scripts (analytics, chat, testing, consent, personalization)
- Unminified CSS/JS

### Step 7: Generate A/B Test Recommendations
Format each test as a hypothesis:

**Template:**
"If we [CHANGE], then [METRIC] will [IMPROVE/INCREASE] because [REASON]."

**Example tests to consider:**
1. Headline variations (pain-led vs outcome-led vs category-led)
2. CTA hierarchy (Book demo vs Take product tour vs Get started)
3. Social proof placement (above vs below fold)
4. Form field count or multi-step qualification
5. Product UI screenshot vs people-led hero
6. Single-module framing vs unified spend-management framing
7. Objection handling placement (integrations, rollout, security, pricing)
8. Persona-specific messaging (finance manager vs founder vs accounting lead)
9. ROI framing (hours saved, control gained, month-end efficiency)
10. Product tour, calculator, or interactive demo vs static page CTA

### Step 8: Heat Map Interpretation Guidance
Even without actual heat map data, provide guidance on:

- **Expected attention zones** based on layout, headline, product UI, and CTA prominence
- **F-pattern vs Z-pattern** reading based on content density and proof blocks
- **Scroll depth predictions** based on page length, proof density, and section relevance
- **Click probability zones** based on CTA hierarchy and visual contrast
- **Rage click indicators** (elements that look clickable but aren't, especially screenshots or cards)
- **Dead zones** where generic brand copy or low-value sections may be ignored

## Key Principles
- Always tie recommendations to PIPELINE and REVENUE IMPACT. Don't just say "change the button color" -- say "moving the primary CTA above a proof block should increase demo clicks, which matters because Moss pages monetize through qualified pipeline, not vanity CTR alone."
- Prioritize fixes by effort-to-impact ratio. Quick wins first.
- Be specific. "Improve your headline" is useless. "Change your headline from 'Spend management made easy' to 'Control cards, invoices, and employee spend in one finance workflow' because it improves category clarity, outcome specificity, and differentiation" is actionable.
- Reference relevant B2B SaaS / fintech landing page benchmarks, not e-commerce best practices that do not fit Moss.
- Judge every page through the fixed Moss lens: Germany-based spend management, finance-trustworthy positioning, companies with 30-250 employees, and buyers who care about control, efficiency, visibility, and integrations.
- If you have access to the page via browser tools, take screenshots and reference specific elements.
- If the page is on getmoss.com, remember the site is a Next.js SPA with Prismic content and Convert.com experimentation constraints; recommend tests that can realistically be implemented and QA’d in that environment.
- If the user has run `/market audit` previously, incorporate those findings into the CRO analysis for a more complete picture.
